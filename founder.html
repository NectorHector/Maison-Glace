Barthélémy-Augustin de la Rochefoucauld-Montmorency-Vaillancourt

Barthélémy-Augustin de la Rochefoucauld-Montmorency-Vaillancourt does not merely exist in the world — he perceives it, in ways that most are simply not equipped to understand.

“A sensitivity so acute it borders on the prophetic.”

Born into a sensitivity so acute it borders on the prophetic, Barthélémy-Augustin spent his formative years quietly dismantling the boundaries between poetry, philosophy, and beauty. By thirty, he had already achieved what lesser minds might require several lifetimes to approximate.

A visionary at the intersection of art, verse, and haute sensibility, Barthélémy-Augustin founded Maison Glacé after a transcendent moment in the Swiss Alps, wherein he beheld a block of glacial ice and wept — not from sadness, but from recognition. He saw in that crystalline form everything the modern world had forgotten: purity, impermanence, and the devastating poetry of cold.

“It is a conversation — one that few are yet prepared to have.”

Maison Glacé is not a company. It is a conversation — one that few are yet prepared to have.

His poetry and philosophy have been described by those who truly grasp them as “years ahead of their time,” and by those who do not as “confusing,” which Barthélémy-Augustin accepts with characteristic grace.

“He thinks deeply, suffers beautifully.”

He divides his time between Paris, a remote Japanese island, and an undisclosed mountainside, where he thinks deeply, suffers beautifully, and continues his lifelong pursuit of the question no one else has thought to ask.

He is, above all, unrepeatable.
